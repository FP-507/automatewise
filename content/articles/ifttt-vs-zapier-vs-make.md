The automation platform market has three dominant players that cover a wide spectrum of needs: IFTTT for personal simplicity, Zapier for business-friendly breadth, and Make for visual power and value. Each platform approaches automation differently, and the right choice depends entirely on what you are trying to accomplish, how technical you are, and how much you are willing to spend.

This three-way comparison breaks down every major dimension so you can make a confident choice. Whether you are automating your smart home, connecting business apps, or building complex multi-step workflows, one of these platforms is the right fit.

## Overview of All Three Platforms

### IFTTT (If This Then That)

IFTTT launched in 2010 as one of the first consumer automation platforms. Its name describes its core model: if this event happens, then do that action. Originally focused entirely on simple, single-trigger, single-action automations called "applets," IFTTT has expanded to support multi-action applets and more complex logic, though it remains the simplest of the three platforms.

IFTTT's unique strength is its deep integration with smart home devices and IoT platforms. It connects to Philips Hue, Ring, Nest, Alexa, Google Home, and hundreds of other hardware devices in ways that Zapier and Make do not.

### Zapier

Zapier is the most widely adopted business automation platform, connecting over 7,000 apps through workflows called "Zaps." It occupies the middle ground between IFTTT's simplicity and Make's complexity, offering multi-step workflows, conditional logic, and advanced features in a step-by-step interface that non-technical users can learn quickly.

### Make (formerly Integromat)

Make is the most powerful visual automation platform of the three. It uses a canvas-based workflow builder where you construct scenarios by connecting modules with lines, creating branches, loops, and error handlers visually. With over 1,800 integrations and powerful data transformation capabilities, Make targets users who need flexibility and control without writing code.

## Feature Comparison Table

Before diving into details, here is a high-level comparison of the three platforms across key dimensions.

### Workflow Complexity

IFTTT supports single-trigger applets with up to 10 actions on paid plans. Logic is limited to filter codes on Pro plans. Zapier supports multi-step Zaps with paths (conditional branches), filters, formatters, and loops. Make supports unlimited branching, iterators, aggregators, error handlers, and complex data transformations on a visual canvas.

### Number of Integrations

IFTTT connects to over 800 services with a strong emphasis on consumer apps and smart home devices. Zapier connects to over 7,000 apps, the largest library in the market. Make connects to over 1,800 apps with deep integration features and an HTTP module for custom connections.

### Builder Interface

IFTTT uses a simple card-based interface where you select a trigger and add actions sequentially. Zapier uses a vertical step-by-step builder with a clean, guided experience. Make uses a horizontal canvas with drag-and-drop modules and visual connections.

### Code Support

IFTTT offers filter code through JavaScript on Pro plans for advanced filtering logic. Zapier offers Code by Zapier steps that run JavaScript or Python. Make offers a built-in function library for data transformation but does not support custom code blocks in the same way.

### Real-Time Execution

IFTTT polls triggers hourly on the free plan and supports near-instant webhook triggers on paid plans. Zapier polls every 1 to 15 minutes depending on your plan, with instant triggers available for supported apps. Make polls every 15 minutes on the free plan and every minute on paid plans, with instant webhooks for supported services.

## Pricing Comparison

Pricing across these three platforms differs significantly in both structure and value.

### IFTTT Pricing

IFTTT's free plan allows two applets with simple trigger-action pairs. IFTTT Pro costs $3.49/month and includes unlimited applets, multi-action applets, filter code, and faster polling. IFTTT Pro Plus costs $14.99/month and adds multiple accounts per service, organization features, and priority support.

IFTTT is by far the most affordable option, but this reflects its simpler feature set. For basic personal automations, the free plan or Pro plan offers excellent value.

### Zapier Pricing

Zapier's free plan includes 100 tasks per month with single-step Zaps only. The Starter plan begins at $19.99/month for 750 tasks with multi-step Zaps. Professional plans add conditional logic and start at $49/month. Team and Company plans add collaboration features at higher price points.

Zapier is the most expensive of the three platforms for equivalent workloads, but the investment reflects the broader integration library and polished experience.

### Make Pricing

Make's free plan includes 1,000 operations per month with two active scenarios. The Core plan starts at $9/month for 10,000 operations with unlimited active scenarios. Higher tiers add team features, priority support, and increased operations.

Make offers the best value at the mid-range. You get more operations per dollar than Zapier and more power than IFTTT. For a comprehensive list of free options across the market, our [best free automation tools](/blog/best-free-automation-tools) guide covers ten platforms with generous free tiers.

## Use Case Suitability

### Personal Automation

For personal automation such as smart home control, fitness tracking integration, social media auto-posting, and personal productivity, IFTTT is the strongest choice. It was designed for exactly these use cases, its smart home and IoT integrations are unmatched, and the pricing is accessible for individual users.

Example personal automations: automatically turn on your lights when you arrive home, save Instagram photos to Google Drive, get a weather forecast every morning via text, or log your daily Fitbit data to a Google Sheet.

Zapier and Make can handle personal automation, but they are over-engineered for simple personal tasks. You would be paying more for features you do not need.

### Small Business Automation

For small businesses connecting CRM, email marketing, project management, invoicing, and communication tools, Zapier and Make are both excellent choices. The right one depends on your priorities.

Zapier is ideal if your business uses niche or industry-specific tools that may not be available on Make. Its 7,000-plus integrations mean almost any SaaS tool in your stack will have a native connection. Zapier is also easier for non-technical team members to learn, which matters when multiple people need to build and maintain automations.

Make is ideal if you need more operations for less money, or if your workflows involve complex data transformation, branching logic, or error handling. A small business processing [automated invoices](/blog/automate-invoice-processing) or running [automated lead generation](/blog/automate-lead-generation) campaigns can save significantly on Make compared to Zapier while accessing more powerful features.

### Agency and Professional Use

Marketing agencies, freelancers, and professional service providers who build automations for clients should strongly consider Make. Its scenario organization, team workspaces, and cloning features make it practical to manage multiple client projects. The visual canvas also makes it easier to document and present workflows to clients.

n8n is also worth considering for this use case. Our [n8n vs Make comparison](/blog/n8n-vs-make) explores the trade-offs between these two power-user platforms.

### Enterprise Automation

For enterprise needs, none of these three platforms is ideal as a standalone solution. Zapier's Team and Company plans offer basic enterprise features, but organizations with serious enterprise requirements should also evaluate Power Automate, especially in Microsoft environments. See our [Power Automate vs Zapier](/blog/power-automate-vs-zapier) comparison for details.

Make's enterprise features are growing, and its EU data residency options appeal to European organizations. IFTTT does not target the enterprise market.

## Complexity and Learning Curve

### IFTTT: Simplest

IFTTT has the lowest learning curve of any automation platform. The concept of "if this, then that" is immediately understandable. Building an applet takes about two minutes, and the interface does not present any options that might confuse a new user.

The trade-off is that IFTTT's simplicity becomes a limitation when you need anything beyond basic trigger-action pairs. Multi-action applets help, but the platform does not support true branching, looping, or error handling.

### Zapier: Moderate

Zapier sits in the sweet spot between simplicity and power. The step-by-step builder is intuitive for beginners, and you can build increasingly complex workflows as you learn concepts like filters, formatters, paths, and lookups. Most users become comfortable with Zapier within a few hours of guided exploration.

The learning curve increases when you start using advanced features like webhooks, Code by Zapier steps, and multi-path Zaps. But the platform provides enough documentation and templates to guide you through each new concept.

### Make: Steepest

Make has the steepest learning curve because its visual canvas and module-based approach require understanding concepts like iterators, aggregators, routes, and data structures before you can build effectively. The initial experience can feel overwhelming compared to Zapier's guided approach.

However, Make's learning curve pays dividends. Once you understand the visual paradigm, building complex workflows becomes faster and more intuitive than on either competing platform. The ability to see your entire automation at a glance, reorganize modules by dragging them, and debug by inspecting individual module outputs makes Make the most productive platform for advanced users.

## Integrations Deep Dive

### IFTTT Integration Strengths

IFTTT excels at consumer and IoT integrations. It connects to smart home platforms (Philips Hue, LIFX, Nest, Ring, Ecobee, SmartThings), voice assistants (Alexa, Google Assistant), fitness trackers (Fitbit, Garmin, Strava), social media (Twitter, Instagram, Facebook, Reddit), and music services (Spotify, SoundCloud).

These integrations are deeply featured for consumer use. You can trigger automations based on arriving at a location, the weather changing, a song being saved, or a specific word appearing in a tweet.

### Zapier Integration Strengths

Zapier excels at business and SaaS integrations. It connects to CRMs (Salesforce, HubSpot, Pipedrive), project management (Asana, Monday.com, ClickUp, Notion), email marketing (Mailchimp, ConvertKit, ActiveCampaign), payment processing (Stripe, PayPal, Square), and hundreds of niche business tools.

The breadth of Zapier's library means you rarely encounter an app that does not have a Zapier integration. This reduces the need for custom API work and makes it practical to automate workflows involving specialized industry tools. For specific workflow ideas, see our guide on [automating social media posting](/blog/automate-social-media-posting).

### Make Integration Strengths

Make's integration library is smaller than Zapier's but larger than IFTTT's. It covers the most popular business tools and adds depth through comprehensive triggers and actions for each connected app. Where Zapier might offer five actions for an app, Make might offer fifteen, giving you more granular control.

Make's HTTP module is also more powerful than the equivalents on other platforms, making it practical to connect to any API-enabled service even without a native integration.

## Performance and Reliability

### Execution Speed

IFTTT is the slowest of the three, with free plan applets running on hourly checks. Paid plans improve this to near real-time for webhook-based triggers, but polling-based triggers can still have noticeable delays.

Zapier processes Zaps quickly once triggered, with paid plans polling every one to two minutes. Instant triggers (available for supported apps) fire within seconds of the triggering event.

Make executes scenarios efficiently, processing all modules in a single run. Paid plans poll every minute, and webhook-based triggers fire instantly. For data-intensive workflows, Make's execution speed is often faster than Zapier's because it processes data more efficiently within each run.

### Reliability

All three platforms maintain high uptime. Zapier and Make both publish status pages and offer SLAs on higher-tier plans. IFTTT is generally reliable but provides less transparency about uptime metrics.

For business-critical automations, Zapier and Make are both strong choices. IFTTT is reliable for personal automations but lacks the enterprise-grade infrastructure guarantees that businesses require.

## Can You Combine These Platforms?

Yes, and it is more common than you might think. Some users run IFTTT for smart home automation while using Zapier or Make for business workflows. Others use Zapier for quick, simple connections and Make for complex data processing workflows.

The main consideration is management overhead. Running automations across multiple platforms means monitoring multiple dashboards, managing multiple subscriptions, and potentially debugging interactions between them. For most users, choosing one primary platform is simpler and more sustainable.

## Conclusion

IFTTT, Zapier, and Make each dominate their respective niches. IFTTT is the best personal and IoT automation platform. Zapier is the best all-around business automation platform for simplicity and breadth. Make is the best platform for power users who need visual complexity and cost efficiency.

Start by identifying your primary use case. If it is personal, go with IFTTT. If it is business and you value simplicity, go with Zapier. If it is business and you value power and value, go with Make. All three offer free plans, so you can test your choice before committing.
