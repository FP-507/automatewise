Choosing between Make (formerly Integromat) and Zapier is one of the most common decisions in the automation world. Both platforms let you connect apps and automate workflows without writing code, but they take fundamentally different approaches to how you build, manage, and scale those automations.

In this detailed comparison, we break down every major aspect of both platforms so you can make a confident decision. Whether you are a solo freelancer automating client work or a growing team looking to streamline operations, this guide will help you find the right fit.

## Platform Overviews

### What Is Zapier?

Zapier launched in 2011 and has grown into the most widely recognized workflow automation platform. It uses a linear, step-by-step approach where you build automations called "Zaps." Each Zap has a trigger (the event that starts the workflow) and one or more actions (the tasks that follow).

Zapier connects to over 7,000 apps, making it the platform with the largest integration library on the market. Its strength lies in simplicity: you can have a working automation running in under five minutes, even if you have never automated anything before.

### What Is Make?

Make, previously known as Integromat, takes a visual-first approach to automation. Instead of linear steps, you build workflows on a drag-and-drop canvas where you can see the entire flow at a glance. Modules (the equivalent of Zapier's steps) connect with lines that show how data moves through your workflow.

Make supports branching, looping, error handling, and parallel processing natively in its visual builder. With over 1,800 integrations and the ability to make custom HTTP requests to virtually any API, Make appeals to users who need more flexibility and control over their automations.

## Pricing Comparison

Pricing is often the deciding factor, and the two platforms structure their plans very differently.

### Zapier Pricing

Zapier uses a task-based pricing model. Every time an action runs in your Zap, that counts as one task. The free plan includes 100 tasks per month with single-step Zaps only. Paid plans start at $19.99/month for 750 tasks and scale up from there. Multi-step Zaps, filters, and advanced logic require a paid plan.

The cost can add up quickly. If you run a Zap that triggers 50 times per day with three actions each, that is 4,500 tasks per month, pushing you toward a higher tier.

### Make Pricing

Make counts operations instead of tasks. Each module execution in your scenario counts as one operation. The free plan gives you 1,000 operations per month, which is already ten times what Zapier offers at the free tier.

Paid plans start at $9/month for 10,000 operations. Even at higher volumes, Make consistently costs less than Zapier for equivalent workloads. For example, running the same 50-trigger, 3-action workflow would cost significantly less on Make because the per-operation pricing is lower.

### The Verdict on Pricing

Make wins on pricing at every tier. If budget is a primary concern, Make offers substantially more value. For teams processing high volumes of data, the savings can be significant over a year. ## Ease of Use

### Zapier's Learning Curve

Zapier was designed for beginners. The interface walks you through building a Zap with clear prompts at every step: choose your trigger app, select the trigger event, connect your account, choose your action app, select the action event, map your data fields, and turn it on. The linear approach means you always know where you are in the process.

For simple, point-to-point automations, Zapier is hard to beat. A non-technical user can automate "when I get a new form submission, add it to my spreadsheet and send me a Slack notification" in about five minutes without any prior experience.

### Make's Learning Curve

Make has a steeper initial learning curve. The visual canvas is powerful but can feel overwhelming at first. You need to understand concepts like modules, connections, routes, and iterators before you can build effectively.

However, once you climb that initial curve, Make's visual approach actually becomes easier for complex workflows. Seeing your entire automation laid out visually makes it much easier to understand, debug, and modify compared to scrolling through a long list of steps in Zapier.

### The Verdict on Ease of Use

For beginners and simple automations, Zapier wins. For power users and complex workflows, Make's visual builder becomes an advantage. If you are just [getting started with automation](/blog/no-code-automation-explained), Zapier's simpler approach may help you build confidence before moving to more advanced platforms.

## Visual Builder Comparison

The workflow builder is where these two platforms differ most dramatically.

### Zapier's Builder

Zapier uses a vertical, step-by-step layout. Each step sits below the previous one. You configure one step at a time, mapping data from previous steps into the current one. Paths (conditional branching) are available on higher plans and display as parallel vertical tracks.

This works well for straightforward sequences but becomes difficult to navigate when workflows grow beyond 10 to 15 steps. You spend a lot of time scrolling up and down to find the right step and check data mappings.

### Make's Builder

Make uses a horizontal canvas where modules appear as circles connected by lines. You can zoom in, zoom out, pan across the canvas, and see the complete workflow architecture at a glance. Branching is intuitive: routes split from a module and run in parallel. You can add error handlers to individual modules, create loops, and aggregate data visually.

The canvas approach also makes it easy to reorganize workflows. You can drag modules to different positions, reroute connections, and restructure without rebuilding from scratch.

### The Verdict on the Builder

Make's visual builder is objectively more powerful and flexible. Zapier's builder is simpler but more limiting. If your workflows involve branching logic, error handling, or data transformation, Make's canvas is the better environment.

## Integration Count and Quality

### Zapier's Integration Library

Zapier dominates here with over 7,000 app integrations. Nearly every SaaS tool you can think of has a Zapier integration, and many apps build their Zapier connector before any other platform. Each integration tends to offer a good range of triggers and actions.

### Make's Integration Library

Make offers around 1,800 native integrations. While this is significantly fewer than Zapier, Make compensates with a powerful HTTP module that lets you connect to any app with an API. For technically inclined users, this means the actual ceiling of what you can connect is nearly unlimited.

Make's integrations also tend to offer deeper functionality within each connected app. Where Zapier might offer 10 actions for an app, Make might offer 20, including more granular control over the data you send and receive.

### The Verdict on Integrations

If you need a specific niche app and want it to work out of the box, Zapier is more likely to have it. If you are comfortable making HTTP requests or your stack uses mainstream tools, Make covers you well and often offers deeper integration with each app.

## Advanced Features

### Data Transformation

Make excels at data manipulation within workflows. You can use built-in functions to parse text, transform dates, calculate values, and restructure JSON without leaving the platform. Zapier offers Formatter steps for similar tasks, but they consume additional tasks and feel less integrated.

### Error Handling

Make provides granular error handling at the module level. You can set break, resume, ignore, rollback, and commit directives for individual modules. Zapier's error handling is more basic: you get notifications when a Zap fails, and you can retry failed tasks, but you cannot build recovery logic into the workflow itself.

### Conditional Logic

Both platforms support conditional branching. Zapier calls them Paths, and they are available on Professional plans and above. Make calls them Routes, and they are available on all plans. Make's implementation is more flexible because you can create unlimited routes from any module, while Zapier limits the number of paths based on your plan.

### Scheduling and Timing

Zapier runs Zaps in near real-time, polling for triggers every 1 to 15 minutes depending on your plan. Make lets you choose your scenario's execution interval, and the free plan polls every 15 minutes while paid plans can run as frequently as every minute. Make also supports scheduling scenarios to run at specific times using a built-in scheduler.

## Performance and Reliability

Both platforms offer high uptime and reliable execution. Zapier processes billions of tasks per month across its user base and has built robust infrastructure to handle scale. Make also delivers strong reliability, though it has experienced occasional slowdowns during peak usage.

One area where Make holds an advantage is execution speed within a workflow. Because Make processes all modules in a scenario in a single execution cycle, complex workflows tend to complete faster than equivalent multi-step Zaps in Zapier, where each step is processed sequentially with slight delays between them.

For high-volume workflows, Make's operation-based counting also means you can process more data per dollar. If you are building [automated report generation](/blog/automate-report-generation) or [data entry workflows](/blog/automate-data-entry) that handle hundreds of records per run, this performance and cost advantage compounds.

## Who Should Use Zapier?

Zapier is the better choice if you fit any of these profiles:

**Non-technical users** who need simple automations running quickly. The guided setup and massive integration library mean you can connect almost any two apps with minimal effort.

**Teams that value ecosystem breadth.** If your tech stack includes niche or industry-specific tools, Zapier is more likely to support them natively.

**Organizations needing enterprise features.** Zapier's higher-tier plans include admin controls, SSO, advanced permissions, and dedicated support that large organizations require.

**Users building mostly linear workflows.** If your automations follow a simple "when this happens, do that" pattern without complex branching, Zapier handles them efficiently.

## Who Should Use Make?

Make is the better choice if you fit any of these profiles:

**Budget-conscious users** who need more operations for less money. Make's pricing is consistently more affordable across all tiers.

**Technical users and power automators** who need visual workflow design, complex branching, error handling, and data transformation within their automations.

**Agencies and freelancers** building automations for clients. Make's scenario organization, cloning, and template features make it easier to replicate and manage workflows across multiple accounts. Our guide on [automation for freelancers](/blog/automation-for-freelancers) covers this in depth.

**Privacy-conscious users.** Make is headquartered in the EU and offers data residency options that matter for GDPR compliance.

**Users processing high data volumes.** Make's operation-based pricing and efficient execution make it more economical for workflows that handle large datasets.

## Migration Between Platforms

If you are currently on one platform and considering switching, know that there is no direct migration path between Zapier and Make. You will need to rebuild your workflows manually on the new platform.

That said, both platforms allow you to export workflow configurations, and several community-built guides exist for translating Zapier Zaps into Make scenarios. The process is straightforward for simple workflows but can be time-intensive for complex ones with many steps and data mappings.

## Conclusion

Both Make and Zapier are excellent automation platforms, but they serve different needs. Zapier is the king of simplicity and integration breadth, making it ideal for beginners and teams that need quick, reliable connections between thousands of apps. Make offers superior visual workflow design, more powerful features, and significantly better pricing, making it the better choice for power users, agencies, and budget-conscious teams.

If you are just starting out, try both free plans. Build a few simple automations on each platform to see which interface and approach resonates with your working style. The best platform is the one you will actually use consistently to [unlock the real benefits of workflow automation](/blog/workflow-automation-benefits).
